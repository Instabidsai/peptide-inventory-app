# Atherosclerosis Overview

Atherosclerosis is a complex condition that involves a biological civil war within your body. It is not merely a plumbing problem, as often described. Instead, it is a multifaceted issue involving the endothelium, the slick lining of your arteries, which is under chronic attack.

## Mechanisms of Atherosclerosis

The primary culprits in this attack are:

- **Sugar**: A significant contributor to endothelial damage.
- **Inflammatory substances**: Often derived from processed foods.
- **Oxidative stress**: This exacerbates the damage to the endothelial lining.

As a result of this damage, the arterial lining becomes sticky, akin to Velcro. Low-Density Lipoprotein (LDL) cholesterol particles, which are typically harmless, drift into the damaged subendothelial space. However, the problem arises when LDL becomes oxidized, turning into oxidized LDL, which acts as a biochemical signal for danger.

## Immune Response and Plaque Formation

The immune system responds to oxidized LDL by sending in monocytes, which transform into macrophages. These macrophages engulf the oxidized LDL, becoming bloated foam cells. Smooth muscle cells and collagen accumulate, forming a fibrous cap over this necrotic area, resulting in plaque that signifies a failure of the body's biochemistry.

## Medical Response to Atherosclerosis

The medical establishment often fails to address the underlying complexities of atherosclerosis. Instead of providing solutions to this multi-system failure, they tend to surrender to the condition without offering effective interventions.

## Conclusion

Understanding atherosclerosis requires a shift in perspective from viewing it as a simple plumbing issue to recognizing it as a complex interplay of biological processes. Addressing the root causes, such as dietary choices and oxidative stress, is crucial for managing and potentially reversing the condition.

## Pharmacological Failures in Medicine

The current medical model often focuses on pharmacological palliative care, managing symptoms rather than addressing root causes. This approach has led to a trillion-dollar industry built on managing chronic conditions through medications that often exacerbate underlying issues.

## Statins and Their Mechanism

Statins, specifically HMG-CoA reductase inhibitors, are commonly prescribed to manage cholesterol levels. However, their mechanism of action is flawed. By inhibiting cholesterol production in the liver, statins can induce a deficiency in coenzyme Q10 (CoQ10), which is crucial for mitochondrial function. This deficiency can lead to muscle pain, weakness, and myopathy, indicating that cells are not receiving the energy they need. Statins also increase insulin resistance and contribute to neurotoxicity, further complicating metabolic health.

## Blood Pressure Medications

Medications such as ACE inhibitors, ARBs, and beta blockers are often prescribed for high blood pressure, which is a symptom of underlying issues such as arterial stiffness and inflammation. These medications do not address the root cause but rather mask symptoms, leading to chronic fatigue and sexual dysfunction as side effects.

## Blood Thinners and Their Risks

Blood thinners, including aspirin and warfarin, are prescribed to prevent clotting in unstable plaque conditions. However, they increase the risk of hemorrhagic strokes and gastrointestinal bleeding, creating a new set of risks while failing to address the underlying plaque instability.

## PCSK9 Inhibitors

PCSK9 inhibitors are monoclonal antibodies that lower LDL cholesterol levels by enhancing the liver's ability to remove LDL from the bloodstream. While effective in reducing LDL levels, they do not address inflammation or oxidative stress and are prohibitively expensive.

3. **Systemic Inflammation**: Chronic low-grade inflammation, characterized by elevated cytokines like IL-6 and TNF-alpha, leads to the oxidation of LDL and contributes to atherosclerosis.
